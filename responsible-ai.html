Algorithm Audit: Policy Stances and Income Inequality
Skills Developed
AI · Machine Learning · Qualitative & Quantitative Research Methodologies · Research Design · Data Collection · Data Analysis

This study investigates how political party policies on economics and social inequality affect income inequality, using the Palma ratio as a measure. The research focuses on the period following the Great Recession and uses a combination of quantitative and qualitative methods. Key tools include the Manifesto Project dataset for analyzing party policies and the UNU-WIDER World Income Inequality Database for income inequality data. A Generalized Estimating Equations (GEE) regression model was developed, complemented by qualitative validation using human and machine (ManifestoBerta) classification of political manifestos. Ethical considerations and the Microsoft Responsible AI Toolbox were employed to address algorithmic biases and ensure fairness.

The study found that while certain economic policy stances, such as welfare state expansion, were statistically significant predictors of the Palma ratio, they alone were insufficient to fully explain income inequality, highlighting the need for additional contextual variables. Biases in data representation across European subregions were addressed using weighting techniques, but persistent variability suggests further refinements are necessary. Qualitative analysis revealed discrepancies between human and machine classifications of political manifestos, driven by local contexts and personal biases, which exposed the limitations of the ManifestoBerta model in accurately reflecting localized political discourse. Auditing with the Microsoft Responsible AI Toolbox highlighted regional and feature-specific variability, underscoring the importance of fairness measures in machine learning models. While bias mitigation slightly improved model performance metrics, significant unexplained variability remains, indicating the need for more nuanced data and methodologies for robust predictions.

This study emphasizes the critical role of addressing algorithmic biases in predicting income inequality and the value of integrating human insights into machine learning models. Policymakers can leverage these findings to design more inclusive and equitable economic policies while ensuring transparency and fairness in AI-driven decision-making processes. Future research should focus on refining models by incorporating broader datasets and local political contexts to enhance predictive accuracy and societal relevance.
